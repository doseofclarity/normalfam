Care of the Normal Family

"The family is the basic unit of the community – healthy families build healthy nations."

The Family in Public Health Nursing

Definition: The family is the basic unit of the community. All members are empowered to maintain their health status, free from disease or infirmity. In public health, the health of the family is considered as a whole and not individually (Page 128).

Why Focus on the Family?

  • Health beliefs, practices, and habits are learned and reinforced within the family.
  • Family members share environment, genetics, and socioeconomic conditions.
  • Health interventions targeted at the family have multiplier effects.
  • The DOH uses a life span approach – health care for specific age groups within the family context.

Family Health Office Goals

  • Improve survival, health & well‑being of mothers and unborn (pre‑pregnancy to postnatal).
  • Reduce morbidity/mortality for children 0‑9 years.
  • Reduce preventable mortality among adolescents and young people.
  • Reduce morbidity/mortality of Filipino adults and older persons.

Other Determinants

  • Genetics – inheritance affects lifespan and disease susceptibility.
  • Personal behavior & coping skills – diet, smoking, physical activity, stress management.
  • Health services – access and use of preventive and curative services.
  • Gender – men and women suffer different diseases at different ages.

Optimum Level of Functioning (OLOF)

The framework refers to OLOF of individuals, families, and communities being influenced by several factors in the eco‑system. The PHN aims to improve family functioning through targeted interventions.

Family Health Assessment

Data Collection Areas (Page 54)

  • Individual: signs/symptoms, medical/nursing history, KAP, coping skills, lifestyle, help‑seeking behavior.
  • Family: structure & characteristics, socioeconomic & cultural factors, environment, health assessment of each member, value placed on prevention, competencies on family health care.
  • Community: population characteristics, physical environment, health/illness data, resources, leadership, culture, participation in health programs.

Data Collection Methods

  • Community surveys
  • Interview of individuals, families, groups
  • Observation of health‑related behaviors
  • Review of records and statistics
  • Physical examination and screening tests
  • Community participation in data gathering

Key Principle:

Assessment is done with active participation of the clients. Data are treated confidentially.

Categories of Health Problems (Page 54)

Health Deficit

Gap between actual and achievable health status. Example: history of repeated infections, no regular health check‑up, presence of illness or disability.

Health Threat

Conditions that promote disease or injury and prevent people from realizing their health potential. Example: inadequate immunization, poor environmental sanitation, exposure to hazards.

Foreseeable Crisis

Stressful occurrences that may precipitate illness or injury. Example: death of a family member, loss of job, natural disaster, or any anticipated stressful life event.

A health need exists when a health problem can be alleviated with medical or social technology, combined with actual/potential resources and commitment to act.

Nursing Process for Family

Steps (Fig. 3, Page 53)

  • Assessment – systematic collection of data.
  • Diagnosis – identification of actual/potential health problems (deficits, threats, crises).
  • Planning – goal setting (SMART: specific, measurable, attainable, realistic, time‑bound).
  • Implementation – nursing interventions, coordination, teaching, referrals.
  • Evaluation – of structure, process, and outcomes.

Planning Considerations

  • Prioritize problems (nature, magnitude, modifiability, preventive potential, salience).
  • Set objectives with client participation.
  • Consider three levels of prevention.
  • Identify resources (family, community, health system).
  • Develop evaluation criteria.

Evaluation Frameworks (Page 57):

  • Structure: physical settings, equipment, staffing, budget.
  • Process: nursing actions, steps of nursing process.
  • Outcome: changes in client's health status, knowledge, attitude, behavior, satisfaction.

Home Visit & Bag Technique

Purposes of Home Visit (Page 61)

  • Give nursing care to sick, postpartum mother & newborn – teach responsible family member.
  • Assess living conditions and health practices.
  • Give health teachings on prevention and control of diseases.
  • Establish close relationship between health agency and public.
  • Promote use of community services and inter‑referral system.

Bag Technique Principles (Page 63)

  • Minimizes spread of infection.
  • Saves time and effort.
  • Shows effectiveness of total care.
  • Bag is placed on a table lined with clean paper (clean side out).
  • Handwashing before and after procedure.
  • Arrange bag contents for efficiency.

Essential contents (Page 62): Thermometers, scissors, forceps, disposable syringes, sterile dressings, cotton balls, cord clamp, plaster, tape measure, sterile gloves, baby scale, alcohol lamp, test tubes, solutions (Betadine, 70% alcohol, Zephiran, hydrogen peroxide, spirit of ammonia, ophthalmic ointment, acetic acid, Benedict's solution). Sphygmomanometer and stethoscope carried separately.

Family Health Programs (Chapter V)

Maternal Health Program

  • Antenatal registration (≥4 visits)
  • Tetanus toxoid immunization
  • Micronutrient supplementation (iron, folic acid, vitamin A)
  • BEMOC strategy
  • Postpartum care

Child Health Programs

  • Exclusive breastfeeding & complementary feeding
  • Newborn screening
  • Expanded Program on Immunization (EPI)
  • Integrated Management of Childhood Illness (IMCI)
  • Micronutrient supplementation (Vitamin A, iron)

Family Planning Program

  • Permanent methods (BTL, vasectomy)
  • Long‑acting (IUD, implant)
  • Short‑acting (pills, injectables, condoms)
  • Natural methods (LAM, BBT, Standard Days, Two‑Day)

Nutrition Program

  • Micronutrient supplementation (Vit A, iron, iodine)
  • Food fortification (RA 8976)
  • Garantisadong Pambata / Child Health Week
  • Home, school & community food production

Also: Adolescent Health, Adult Men/Women Health, Older Person Program, Reproductive Health Framework (10 elements).

PHN Role in Normal Family Care

Primary Prevention

Health promotion & specific protection: health education, immunization, family planning, nutrition promotion, environmental sanitation.

Secondary Prevention

Early diagnosis & prompt treatment: screening (BP, diabetes, cancer), case finding, prenatal check‑ups, well‑child clinics.

Tertiary Prevention

Rehabilitation & disability limitation: home care for chronic illness, support groups, physical therapy, counseling.

  • Health educator: Teach family on nutrition, hygiene, immunization, family planning, breastfeeding, child care.
  • Care provider: Nursing interventions during home visits, clinic visits, school visits.
  • Manager/Coordinator: Organize family health programs, supervise midwives/BHWs, manage referrals.
  • Advocate: Empower family to access health services and adopt healthy behaviors.
  • Researcher: Participate in community diagnosis and health surveillance.
  • Role model: Demonstrate healthy lifestyle practices.
